## The Working Model

Here is the model I recommend for **human review patterns for AI workflows**:

- keep the first version small enough to test in one sitting
- prefer defaults that make rollback obvious
- add observability before scale forces you to
- document the exact failure signs you care about
- push complexity out until the simple version genuinely hurts

That model keeps the article practical and stops it from turning into another abstract best-practices page.

## How to Keep AI Output Useful

Start with a thin slice: one input, one expected output shape, one grading rule, and one manual review path. That is enough to catch most failures before they hit users. For workflows that generate structured output, keep one golden dataset of prompts and expected fields. For review-driven flows, define when the human must see the result, when the model can auto-complete, and what confidence or rule breach should trigger fallback. The mistake to avoid is chasing benchmark theater. Good evals are small, local, boring, and tied to your actual product surface.

## Watch Out For

Do not add human review everywhere. Add it where the output can create support load, legal risk, or irreversible action.
