If you want the short answer, here it is: use `next-mdx-remote/rsc` when your MDX files behave like content data in a real posts system, and use `@next/mdx` when the MDX files themselves are route-level pages inside the App Router. Most wasted time happens when people mix those two mental models and then blame MDX.

That is why this comparison matters. This is not a generic MDX overview. It is a practical Next.js 15 decision page for developers choosing a content architecture before they build, or while they are debugging a route that already went sideways in production.

If your setup is already broken with a runtime mismatch, jump straight to [Fix next-mdx-remote/rsc Edge Error in Next.js 15](/blog/nextjs-mdx-remote-rsc-edge-runtime-fix). If you still need the full file-backed implementation, start with [Next.js MDX Blog in 2026](/blog/nextjs-mdx-blog-2026). If the implementation already works and you are trying to harden it with syntax highlighting, feeds, sitemap, and metadata, follow it with [Next.js MDX Blog with Syntax Highlighting, RSS, Sitemap, and SEO](/blog/nextjs-mdx-blog-syntax-highlighting-rss-sitemap-seo).

<Callout type="warning" title="Decision">
Choose `next-mdx-remote/rsc` for file-backed blogs and content workflows. Choose `@next/mdx` for route-level MDX pages inside `app`. If your content needs frontmatter, archives, related posts, and feed or sitemap support, the file-backed model is the better fit.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="TL;DR">
If your MDX files live under `content/posts` and flow through a posts library, choose `next-mdx-remote/rsc`. If your MDX files are actual route content inside `app`, choose `@next/mdx`. The wrong choice usually shows up later as awkward content structure or runtime pain.
</Callout>

## Decision table

| Dimension | `next-mdx-remote/rsc` | `@next/mdx` |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Best for | file-backed blogs and content systems | route-level MDX pages inside `app` |
| Content source | MDX files under `content/posts` | MDX files that are the route |
| Rendering model | content data rendered by a posts layer | native MDX page rendering |
| Editorial control | stronger, easier to add metadata and archives | lighter, fewer layers |
| Setup complexity | higher because you maintain a posts library | lower because the route is the content |
| Long-term fit | better for publishing workflows | better for docs or small MDX sections |

## What this comparison is actually deciding

The real decision is not "which library renders markdown better?" Both can render MDX. The real decision is where your content lives and how the rest of the app thinks about it.

If your blog has:

- `content/posts/*.mdx`
- frontmatter parsing
- `getAllPosts()` and `getPostBySlug()`
- category pages
- related posts
- sitemap and RSS driven from the same content model

then your MDX acts like content data. That is the world where `next-mdx-remote/rsc` usually fits better.

If your app has route files like `app/docs/getting-started/page.mdx` and the MDX file itself is the page, then `@next/mdx` is often the cleaner fit. That is not a better renderer. It is a different content model.

## When `next-mdx-remote/rsc` is the better choice

`next-mdx-remote/rsc` wins when your content belongs to a posts system instead of the route tree.

That usually means:

- blog posts live outside `app`
- a posts library reads files and parses frontmatter
- the route receives prepared content by slug
- the same content model drives archive pages, related posts, RSS, and sitemap

That is exactly why it fits the stack in [Next.js MDX Blog in 2026](/blog/nextjs-mdx-blog-2026). The route stays focused on rendering. Your content logic stays in one place. That separation makes it much easier to build categories, reading time, featured images, related-post logic, and metadata without turning route files into a giant content management layer.

If you are building a file-backed developer blog, I would still choose `next-mdx-remote/rsc` first. It matches the mental model better.

## When `@next/mdx` is the better choice

`@next/mdx` is better when your MDX files are really part of the route tree.

That usually means:

- each MDX file behaves like a page
- content lives under `app`
- you want first-party route-level MDX support
- you are not building a heavy posts library around every page

This is cleaner for docs sections, product pages, or small content areas that act more like part of the app than part of a publishing system. In that shape, `@next/mdx` feels more direct because the content file and route structure line up naturally.

The mistake is forcing `@next/mdx` into a blog that really wants a richer posts system. You can do it, but you will spend more time rebuilding features that come naturally once content is treated like data.

## How file-backed content changes the decision

This is the section most comparisons miss.

File-backed content changes everything because the moment you have `content/posts/*.mdx`, your architecture stops being about route rendering alone. You now care about:

- slug lookup
- frontmatter extraction
- sorting and filtering
- category pages
- related content
- archive generation
- sitemap and RSS

That is why file-backed content usually pushes the decision toward `next-mdx-remote/rsc`. The content is not the route. The route is only one consumer of the content.

If your plan is "I want a blog repo that behaves like application code," the pattern in [Next.js MDX Blog in 2026](/blog/nextjs-mdx-blog-2026) is still the stronger fit. If your plan is "I want a few MDX pages inside the app," `@next/mdx` is usually enough.

## Runtime implications in Next.js 15

This is where people accidentally think the library choice is broken when the real problem is the runtime.

With `next-mdx-remote/rsc`, a file-backed content path often touches Node.js APIs through things like:

- `fs`
- `path`
- frontmatter parsing
- server-side content loaders

That is exactly why the dedicated troubleshooting page exists: [Fix next-mdx-remote/rsc Edge Error in Next.js 15](/blog/nextjs-mdx-remote-rsc-edge-runtime-fix). The issue is usually not that `next-mdx-remote/rsc` is wrong. The issue is that the route inherited Edge while the content path still needed Node.js runtime.

`@next/mdx` can avoid some of that pain when the content is route-level and the architecture is simpler. But that does not make it automatically better. It just means the runtime story is different because the content model is different.

## App Router behavior: where each approach feels natural

In App Router, `@next/mdx` feels natural when the route tree is the content structure. A page-like MDX file under `app` stays conceptually simple.

`next-mdx-remote/rsc` feels natural when App Router is just the rendering layer over a content system. The route can stay slim while `lib/posts.ts` does the heavy lifting.

That is the practical distinction:

- `@next/mdx` fits route-first content
- `next-mdx-remote/rsc` fits content-first architecture

If you confuse those two, the project starts fighting you later. Not on day 1. Later, when you want related posts, feeds, categories, and stronger metadata.

## When people accidentally choose the wrong one

The most common wrong choice is picking `@next/mdx` because it feels more "official," then realizing the project actually wants a real posts library with frontmatter helpers, archive pages, and content queries. At that point, route-level MDX starts to feel too literal.

The second common wrong choice is picking `next-mdx-remote/rsc` for a small set of MDX pages that really could have lived inside `app` with less machinery.

The third common wrong choice is blaming the library when the runtime is the real issue. If your route only fails after deploy, stop arguing about abstractions and read [Fix next-mdx-remote/rsc Edge Error in Next.js 15](/blog/nextjs-mdx-remote-rsc-edge-runtime-fix) before you rewrite the whole stack.

## Which one should you use?

I would use `next-mdx-remote/rsc` for a real file-backed blog in Next.js 15. I have already seen that pattern stay cleaner once the content model grows past simple rendering and starts needing metadata, feeds, related content, and archive behavior.

The exception is straightforward. If your MDX is genuinely route-level content inside `app`, `@next/mdx` is cleaner because it matches the shape of the app better.

So my practical rule is this:

- use `next-mdx-remote/rsc` for content-driven blog architecture
- use `@next/mdx` for route-driven MDX pages

The bad outcome is not choosing either library. The bad outcome is choosing the one that fights your content model.

## FAQ

### Is `next-mdx-remote/rsc` better than `@next/mdx` in Next.js 15?

For a file-backed blog, usually yes. It fits a posts library model better. For route-level MDX pages inside `app`, `@next/mdx` can be the cleaner choice. The right answer depends on how your content is structured, not on which package sounds more official.

### Should I use `next-mdx-remote/rsc` for a file-based blog?

Yes, in most cases. If posts live on disk and you want helpers like slug lookup, frontmatter parsing, archives, related posts, and feed generation, `next-mdx-remote/rsc` usually matches that architecture better.

### Does `@next/mdx` work better with App Router?

It works more naturally when your MDX files are route-level pages under `app`. That is the key distinction. It is not automatically better for blogs with a separate content layer.

### Does `next-mdx-remote/rsc` require Node.js runtime?

Not always, but file-backed uses often end up depending on Node.js APIs through the surrounding content path. That is why Edge runtime mistakes show up so often with that pattern.

### Can `@next/mdx` replace a posts library?

Not really if your site needs a proper content system. You can still build helpers around it, but the moment you need sorting, archives, categories, feeds, and richer metadata, the architecture starts looking different from simple route-level MDX.

### When should I switch from `next-mdx-remote/rsc` to `@next/mdx`?

Switch only when your content model is actually route-first. If the content still behaves like data in a blog system, swapping libraries usually adds churn without solving the real problem.

### What should I read if `next-mdx-remote/rsc` breaks only after deploy?

Start with [Fix next-mdx-remote/rsc Edge Error in Next.js 15](/blog/nextjs-mdx-remote-rsc-edge-runtime-fix). That is the narrow troubleshooting path for runtime inheritance and production-only failures.
