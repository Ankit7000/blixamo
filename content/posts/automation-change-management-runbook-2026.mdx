# Automation Change Management Runbook

Most automation breakage comes from sloppy change control, not exotic bugs. This runbook is for teams that already have workflows live and need a calmer way to version, approve, roll back, and inspect changes. It adds the governance layer missing from the current Automation cluster.

## The Safe Sequence

For most teams, the safest sequence is:

1. inventory what exists now  
2. capture configs, secrets, and paths  
3. make one reversible change at a time  
4. verify the result with a real request or restore test  
5. keep the rollback step ready until traffic has settled

This sounds basic, but it is where most avoidable outages live. People skip the inventory, assume defaults, or call a migration “done” before they test the ugly paths like webhooks, uploads, cron jobs, or background workers.

## How to Ship Workflow Changes Safely

The strongest automation teams ship workflows the same way they ship code: with test data, rollback expectations, and a clear owner. Keep staging inputs separate from production credentials. Log every external call that can fail. Add one canary path or dry-run mode where possible. The moment you cannot answer “what changed?” after a workflow starts misbehaving, you already have a change-management problem. This article fixes that with a lightweight operator habit, not enterprise ceremony.

## Watch Out For

Do not let workflow edits hit production without a named owner and a rollback note.

### Who is this article for?

### Is this a beginner topic or an ops topic?

### What makes this different from a broad roundup?

### What should I verify before publishing this change?
